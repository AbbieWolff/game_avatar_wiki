Case Study: Presence & Agency

Background
When a player is highly immersed in a virtual world, it is possible to create confusion about agency for actions taken by an avatar, after which the player is left to wonder whether they instigated and caused an action of the avatar or not.1

A University of Barcelona study examined this phenomenon by making participants feel as though they had body ownership of an avatar.
Method
With a combination of motion sensing the participants and subsequent movement replication by the avatar, participants were led to believe that the 3D avatar they viewed in a virtual mirror was a reflection of their own body. The avatar then said "casa" (the Spanish word for home/house), with accompanying vibrations partcipants' vocal chords.
Result
The participant could not determine whether they chose to speak the word or whether the avatar spoke the word of its own accord. This was the intention of the researchers, to create identity and agency confusion.

These results have definite social implications and signal some promising--and frightening--future uses of virtual reality and avatars.

This technology can demonstrably, at least in specific situations in university laboratories, offer an opportunity for players/participants to swap selves and explore other identities almost literally. Internal racism, sexism, self-esteem, and many other social issues and psychological problems can be, at least temporarily, lessened through virtual reality identity exploration. 1

This illusion of body ownership can lead to negative psychological effects and sometimes emotional suffering if participants/players are led to feel ownership for actions they did not choose and/or did not agree to perform.
