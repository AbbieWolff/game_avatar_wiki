Anonymity

Anonymous vs. Identifiable Avatars
Primarily, anonymity has to do with whether the player feels anonymous. There are many ways a player could potentially be identified (for example, since her activity is tied to an IP address); however, the technical sophistication of the user affects her feeling of anonymity.

Players may not feel anonymous if:
  • They have persistent identities within or across virtual worlds and game sites
  • go by their real name online
  • otherwise identify themselves publicly in a way that connects to their real-world
  • create an avatar that looks like them 1
Real Benefits
Anonymous avatars are often used by players as a means of identity exploration—especially by gamers who identify with the LGBTQ+? community.2

Online anonymity provides an opportunity for players to try on new identities that they may not be comfortable exploring offline, while avoiding real-world *social consequences*.

Escaping from their offline identities also allows players to explore, and, eventually, understand and accept themselves. Online communities can facilitate identity demarginalization through greater self-acceptance, decreased estrangement from society and reduction of social isolation. 2
Virtual misbehavior
Most visitors to the comment sections of certain websites (like YouTube) and online games (like Call of Duty) can attest to this dark side of anonymity.

Anonymity can cause problems during virtual teamwork. Research shows that virtual teams work better, in general, when members are identifiable, not anonymous. Virtual teams report less social loafing and more credibility and accountability when the team members’ avatars are identifiable.1

Similarly, anonymous users can be more likely to engage in harassment or otherwise misbehave in virtual environments. Anonymous players don't care about social norms because they do not suffer any *social consequences*
