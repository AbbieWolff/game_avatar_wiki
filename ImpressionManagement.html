Self-Representation & Impression Management

Definition
As in real life, players in virtual worlds manage how they represent themselves to other people through strategic choices in avatar appearance and behavior.

Social norms can strongly influence the most common methods and styles of self-representation and impression management. Harmful social norms, such as harassment, can also influence the ways that players choose to self-represent.

In addition to the avatar’s appearance, virtual goods/accessories can be part of the image people present. However, anonymity and immersion impact the perceived emotional and social value of the decorative virtual goods.1

Self-Awareness
Some players consciously manage their impressions on others.

Sometimes, participants opt to represent themselves accurately, both in their behavior and their avatar's appearance . However, most players maintain at least slight exaggeration of reality in favor of representing themselves better. Some even take on extremely different identities.2

Since people are not always representing themselves accurately, some see this as a form of deception.3 Whether these improvements are truly more deceptive than real life self-representation is up for debate.3

Avatar Similarity
Players with avatars that share some visual similarity with other members of their virtual social group can experience positive effects from that similarity, including3:
  • Heightened self-awareness
  • Greater self-presence
  • Stronger Player-Avatar Identification
Age, Ethnicity, & Gender Disclosure
In virtual realities, it is much easier to hide one’s identity by choosing avatars who appear the way players want to be seen, even if it is not similar to the way they actually look in real life.

However, disclosing one’s real age, ethnicity and gender, especially when players’ are a minority group, can be fraught with social risks, even in virtual worlds.4

As in real-world social situations, some gamers are cruel to players from minority groups by engaging in rassment, ostracism, and/or microaggressions in-game.? These behaviors are often motivated by internal racism, sexism, ageism, etc. or because they are social norms in the group.

As a result, gamers who identify as female or genderqueer, who are from ethnic minorities, and/or who are older than the majority of other players may hesitate to disclose these details.5

Virtual worlds that provide more equitable diversity representation than the current norm can create a safer environment for players who may otherwise hesitate to disclose such traits.
