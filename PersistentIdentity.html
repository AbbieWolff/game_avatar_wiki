Persistent Identity

Definition
Persistent identity defines how a person usually represents him/herself. It includes personality traits, preferences and opinions, typical patterns of behavior, and everything else that makes up what people think of as their identity.

Persistent identity is, in other words, "a fixed set of identifiers...associated with a person so that he/she can be recognized or distinguished by others and by society in general."1

Importance
In virtual worlds, players have the opportunity to create many different alternate identities -- each represented by a unique avatar.

It is necessary for both psychological well-being and relationship formation in virtual worlds to maintain a persistent primary identity that people recognize and associate with an avatar.1 Generally, it's not socially acceptable to drastically change how a player represents him/herself.

  • To prevent players from re-experiencing the uncomfortable sensation of identity confusion that almost everyone endures during adolescence.
  • In social situations in virtual worlds, to distinguish between who one actually is and who one is presenting as while playing each avatar. Without this distinction between who a player is and who/what they are playing as, it is hard to engender trust and thus to form social relationships.2
Projection
Players project some aspects of their persistent identities onto their avatars in social virtual environments.

Players with high levels of agreeableness on personality measurements were more likely to direct their avatars to engage in pro-social behaviors in Massively Multiplayer Online Games (MMOGs) than players with low levels of measured agreeableness.2 3

Agreeableness is defined as the “interpersonal tendencies related to self-interest, concern for others, and social harmony".4

Along the same Big Five personality measure, players with high levels of introversion and neuroticism--especially those with low self-esteem--create avatars that visually differ more from their own physical characteristics than players with high self-esteem.
Arguably, this is done to manage others’ impressions by making their avatar's behavior appear more extroverted, less neurotic, and more confident.1
