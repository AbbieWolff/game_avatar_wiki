Virtual Reality Therapy

Overview
Virtual worlds like Second Life have become new frontiers for virtual therapy for patients with a range of psychological issues, such as addiction, anxiety, PTSD, depression, and body image.

While many patients have found virtual reality therapy helpful and convenient, its format can create unique problems. The method of therapy, whether through "text, voice, video or avatar (or in-person)" can offer patients more objectivity through psychological distance from the avatar. This objectivity can be beneficial, but it can also mitigate the efficacy of the virtual reality therapy. 1

Additionally, there is a risk of patients swapping one issue for another: presenting issues can be replaced with or superseded by a burgeoning internet or video gaming addiction through the use of virtual reality therapy.1

This treatment method is still very new, so it will take time to understand all of its social and psychological implications.1

Body Image
Researchers found that users' body image can be demonstrably influenced by self-created, but not random, avatars in virtual reality.

Playing as a self-created "ideal" avatar seemed to motivate positive health behavior change, but being in a risky situation while playing as that avatar was demotivating.2

Body dissatisfaction often results from a perceived discrepancy between a person's actual self and ideal self. It can result in negative emotional states and health outcomes.2

Based on these results, the researchers encouraged companies and groups to promote positive health behaviors in video games and other virtual worlds, rather than trying to scare patients into avoiding negative behaviors. Fear is not a good motivator.2
